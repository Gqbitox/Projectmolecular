What is the Telomers?

Principal function

Protect the structural integrity of chromosomes and maintain genomic stability. During DNA replication, the replication machinery is unable to complete synthesis at the 3' end of the chromosome, leading to progressive shortening of telomeres with each cycle of cell division.

Telomers

Telomeres are repetitive DNA sequences located at the ends of linear chromosomes in eukaryotic cells. They are mainly composed of TTAGGG sequences in mammals and are associated with specific proteins that form a complex structure called telomeric protein complex.

Aging

This progressive shortening is associated with cellular aging, where cells reach a state of senescence and stop dividing. Decreased telomere length can also trigger DNA repair responses or induce apoptosis to prevent the proliferation of cells with genome damage.

Maintenance

The maintenance of telomere length is regulated by enzymes called telomerases, which add DNA sequences to the ends of telomeres to compensate for their shortening during cell division. Telomerase activity is tightly controlled and its regulation is crucial for cellular homeostasis and longevity.

In a normal cell, telomeres naturally shorten with each cell division, but cells have mechanisms to regulate this shortening and maintain telomere integrity in a functional range. Guanine, a key part of telomeres, forms a protective loop-shaped structure that guards the ends of chromosomes. This structure prevents cells from recognizing the ends as damaged, allowing cells to continue dividing without activating cell arrest or death responses.

However, oxidative stress can damage these guanine sequences in telomeres. Free radicals generated during oxidative stress can oxidize guanines, weakening the protective structure of telomeres. This damage can accelerate telomere shortening, leading to cellular responses such as senescence or death.

Guanine's vulnerability to oxidation makes oxidative stress especially detrimental to telomere integrity. This relationship between oxidative stress, guanine in telomeres and the resulting shortening is fundamental in cellular aging and can influence the overall health of cells.

Do telomeres "oxidize" as we age?

Guanine is the most susceptible of the natural bases to oxidation, commonly generating 8-oxoguanine (8-oxoG).

8-oxoG was found at telomeres in comparison to microsatellite repeats and bulk genomic DNA after cellular exposures to oxidizing agents.

TTAGGG repeats are preferred sites for iron binding and iron-mediated Fenton reactions.
